Chart Selection Justifications
1. Sales Trend View (Line Chart)
Question it answers: How are sales changing over time?
Why I picked a line chart: Line charts are just the best for showing trends over time. You can instantly see if sales are going up, down, or staying flat month by month. It makes the story super clear.
Fields used:
X-Axis: Order Date (by Month)
Y-Axis: Sales
Filters: Region, Category, Customer Segment, Date
Design choices: I kept it simple — one clean color line and clear labels so nothing distracts from the trend.
What I avoided: I didn’t use a bar chart because bars are clunky for continuous time trends.
2. Regional Performance (Bar Chart)
Question it answers: Which regions are doing better in terms of profit?
Why a bar chart: Bars are perfect for comparing values side by side. You can immediately spot which regions are crushing it and which ones need attention.
Fields used:
X-Axis: Profit
Y-Axis: Region
Color: Profit (green for positive, red for negative)
Labels: Profit values
Design choices: The color coding (green = good, red = bad) makes it really easy to read at a glance.
What I avoided: No pie chart — pies are terrible for comparing exact values.
3. Category Profitability (Horizontal Bar Chart)
Question it answers: Which product categories and sub-categories are the most and least profitable?
Why horizontal bars: When you have lots of category and sub-category names, horizontal bars keep the text readable and make comparisons easy.
Fields used:
Y-Axis: Category + Sub-Category
X-Axis: Profit
Color: Profit
Labels: Profit values
Design choices: I sorted the bars by profit so the winners and losers are obvious right away.
What I avoided: Too many colors or a treemap — those make it harder to compare precise numbers.
4. Customer Segment View (Bar Chart)
Question it answers: Which customer segment brings in the most sales?
Why a bar chart: It’s the quickest way to compare segments and see who the biggest contributors are.
Fields used:
X-Axis: Customer Segment
Y-Axis: Sales
Color: Different color per segment
Design choices: Distinct colors help each segment stand out without making it messy.
What I avoided: Extra labels and decorations — kept it clean and focused.
5. Shipping Performance (Bar Chart)
Question it answers: Which shipping modes are the fastest and slowest?
Why a bar chart: Great for comparing average delivery days across different shipping categories.
Fields used:
X-Axis: Ship Mode
Y-Axis: Average Delivery Days
Color: Shipping Delay Bucket
Design choices: Simple colors and straightforward labels.
What I avoided: Line charts — because shipping modes are categories, not a time series.
6. Discount vs Profit (Scatter Plot)
Question it answers: How does discount level affect profit?
Why a scatter plot: It’s the perfect chart for exploring the relationship between two numerical variables. You can clearly see if higher discounts are hurting profits.
Fields used:
X-Axis: Discount
Y-Axis: Profit
Color: Category
Detail: Product Name
Design choices: Color by category helps separate the data while keeping things readable.
What I avoided: Bar charts — they wouldn’t show the correlation/relationship nearly as well.
7. Return Analysis (Bar Chart)
Question it answers: Which categories have the highest product return rates?
Why a bar chart: Simple and effective for comparing return rates across categories.
Fields used:
X-Axis: Return Rate
Y-Axis: Category
Color: Return Rate
Design choices: Clean layout with clear labels so the problem areas immediately stand out.
What I avoided: Clutter — only the essential info is shown.
Overall Dashboard Design
I wanted the whole dashboard to feel clean and user-friendly. I grouped related charts together, put the key KPI cards at the top for quick insights, and added filters on the side so people can easily drill down. I stuck to a consistent color scheme and avoided flashy stuff that doesn’t add value. The goal was to make it professional but still easy for anyone in the business to understand and use.
